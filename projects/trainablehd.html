Abstract

The paper is really about removing the two assumptions that kept classic HD learning stuck: the encoder stays random, and the update rule stays simplistic.

Conventional hyperdimensional classifiers usually spend their learning budget on class hypervectors while leaving the encoder fixed after random initialization. That decision makes representation quality an accident of initialization. If the encoder does a poor job separating features, the only remaining escape valve is to increase dimensionality, retrain longer, or tolerate a wider gap to deep neural networks.

TrainableHD changes that structure directly. It learns the base hypervectors that define the encoder, couples those updates with adaptive optimizers such as Adam and Adagrad, and then carries the model through quantization-aware training so the same learned representation can run efficiently on CPUs, low-power GPUs, and FPGAs. The contribution is not just better accuracy. It is a cleaner accuracy-efficiency frontier for HDC.

Motivation

Three bottlenecks explain why TrainableHD matters beyond a small accuracy tweak.

The paper is strong because it does not treat HDC accuracy as an isolated modeling issue. It connects representation quality, optimizer choice, and hardware execution into one training story.

Frozen encoders cap representation quality

Earlier HD methods project inputs with fixed random base hypervectors. That keeps inference simple, but it also locks in weak feature relationships. TrainableHD makes the base hypervectors learnable so the encoder can move toward the task instead of remaining a random front-end forever.

Static learning rates waste HDC's fast-learning behavior

HDC typically converges quickly, which means the best update policy can vary sharply across epochs and datasets. The paper brings in adaptive optimizers to tune update magnitude and direction instead of relying on a single fixed rule that may be too aggressive early or too weak later.

Efficiency only matters if the learned model still maps cleanly to hardware

Improving accuracy is not enough if the result depends on expensive floating-point execution. TrainableHD therefore adds quantization-aware training, encoder interval training, and drift-aware fake quantization so the learned model stays practical on edge-class accelerators.

Pipeline

The core of TrainableHD is a learned encoder wrapped in optimizer-aware and hardware-aware training.

The method becomes clearer if you separate the algorithm into representation learning, update scheduling, and deployment preparation. All three are necessary for the final result.

Step 01
Encode samples with trainable base hypervectors
Instead of relying on a permanently random encoder, TrainableHD treats the base hypervectors as parameters. Training samples are still encoded into feature hypervectors and compared against class hypervectors in the usual HD way, but now the encoder itself can adapt when the model makes mistakes.
Step 02
Translate sample-wise classification error into encoder updates
The framework first updates class hypervectors, then uses the same error information to estimate why the encoding failed at the feature level. That lets TrainableHD modify the base hypervectors directly and learn better hyperspace structure without adding extra work to inference.
Step 03
Use adaptive optimizers to match HDC's rapid convergence
The paper studies SGD, Adagrad, AdaDelta, RMSProp, Momentum, NAG, and Adam. The important systems point is that HDC training behaves differently from deep networks: Adagrad is attractive when retraining is brief, while Adam gives the best overall inference accuracy after longer training.
Step 04
Reduce training overhead with encoder interval training and drift-aware update
Encoder Interval Training caches encoded feature hypervectors and reuses them for several iterations before re-encoding, while Drift-Aware Update skips fake quantization until the accumulated parameter drift becomes meaningful. Together they target the two places where a stronger training rule would otherwise become costly.
Step 05
Deploy the final model in INT8 on practical accelerators
Quantization-aware training prepares the learned base and class hypervectors for low-precision execution. After training, inference can run entirely in the INT8 domain on CPU SIMD paths, Tensor-Core-capable GPUs, or FPGA implementations, which is where the paper's efficiency argument becomes concrete.

Why the dynamic encoder is the real conceptual shift

Most HDC work improves the classifier while treating encoding as infrastructure. TrainableHD moves the learning problem upstream. Once the encoder becomes trainable, lower dimensions start becoming viable because the representation itself improves instead of only the readout layer.

Why the optimization tricks are part of the contribution

EIT and DAU are not side utilities. They are what keep the stronger training loop aligned with HDC's original value proposition. Without them, the method would gain quality but give back too much of the lightweight edge that motivated HDC in the first place.

Results

The reported gains are strongest where they should matter most: low dimension, low power, and real deployment hardware.

The paper does not claim that TrainableHD beats every baseline in every possible sense. It claims something more useful: that HDC can move materially closer to DNN-level quality while remaining much cheaper to train and serve on modest platforms.

What the paper shows about quality and efficiency across the main baselines

Comparison Reported evidence Why it matters
TrainableHD vs baseline HDC Average accuracy improves by 7.02% at D = 3,000 and 4.86% at D = 10,000. On HACT, the paper reports 85.27% at D = 3,000 versus 57.28% for baseline, and 86.16% versus 59.35% at D = 10,000. The gain is not coming from simply raising dimension. The encoder itself is learning a better feature geometry.
TrainableHD vs ManiHD At D = 3,000, TrainableHD reports a 9.68% average accuracy gain over ManiHD and still improves inference efficiency by 3.3x in FP32 and 16.1x in INT8. The paper argues that learning quality should not depend on expensive manifold-projection overhead at deployment time.
TrainableHD vs DNN on edge hardware On Jetson Xavier, TrainableHD is 56.4x faster and 73x more energy efficient than the DNN baseline, with an additional 3.1x performance improvement after quantization. This is the practical claim: the model is not only lighter in theory, but concretely better for low-power inference.
TrainableHD under aggressive dimension reduction Average accuracy loss from D = 10,000 to D = 500 is only 0.99%, and on MNIST the paper reports 96.90% at D = 1,000 versus the baseline's 97.34% at D = 10,000. That makes dimension a controllable systems tradeoff rather than a fixed tax on accuracy.

Adam wins overall, but the optimizer story is nuanced

Adam gives the best aggregate inference quality and improves average accuracy by 1.06% over the authors' earlier version. But the paper also notes that Adagrad is attractive when retraining time is short, which is useful for online or budget-limited HDC deployments.

EIT cuts the expensive part of dynamic encoding without collapsing accuracy

EIT reduces time spent on base updates by 89.20% and on encoding by 69.89%. With suitable optimizers, the average loss stays small: 0.43% for Adagrad and 0.73% for Adam in the reported evaluation.

The systems contribution is really about improving HDC's usable operating range

TrainableHD is important because it makes several desirable regimes overlap at once: smaller dimensions, low-precision inference, fast training, and higher accuracy. That combination is what makes the page stronger than a standard algorithm-only summary.

Takeaways

Why this page matters inside CELL's HD research line.

  1. Point 01
    This paper upgrades HDC from a lightweight but mostly fixed representation pipeline into a learnable representation system. That is the conceptual bridge between older symbolic efficiency and newer data-driven adaptability.
  2. Point 02
    The deployment details are central, not decorative. Quantization-aware training, EIT, and DAU explain why the method remains plausible for edge devices after making the encoder stronger.
  3. Point 03
    For the website, TrainableHD should read as the paper that makes dynamic HD learning operational: better accuracy, better low-dimension behavior, and stronger hardware alignment all in one place.
