Abstract

The paper is really about fixing the weakest assumption in classic HD learning: that the encoder can stay frozen forever.

Standard hyperdimensional learning pipelines spend their effort on class hypervectors, while the encoder that projects input data into hyperspace usually stays static. That design choice makes the learned representation brittle: if the projection is poor, the training process can only compensate by using more dimensions and more retraining on the class vectors.

This paper proposes two forward-only alternatives for dynamic encoder training. Stochastic Error Projection (SEP) perturbs the projection matrix according to the observed classification error without backpropagation, while Input Modulated Projection (IMP) performs a second forward pass with an error-modulated input and updates the encoder from the change in activation. Both methods keep the training biologically motivated, avoid the weight-transport assumptions of backpropagation, and still reach state-of-the-art HD performance at much lower dimensionality.

Motivation

Three limitations of conventional HD training are driving the redesign.

The paper is useful because it does not just say “dynamic encoders are better.” It explains why static encoding creates both accuracy and hardware problems.

Static encoders create suboptimal representations

Popular encoders such as ID-level, linear, and nonlinear projection stay fixed after initialization. If those projections are poorly aligned to the data, the training loop can only patch the problem indirectly through class hypervector updates.

Backprop-style HD training is expensive and biologically awkward

TrainableHD showed that dynamic encoders help, but it relies on a backpropagation-like mechanism and per-feature error updates. The paper positions SEP and IMP as more forward-only, lightweight alternatives.

Poor encoding forces unnecessarily high dimensions

When the encoder is weak, HD systems compensate by using very wide hypervectors such as 10,000 dimensions. That hurts memory footprint and inference efficiency even if the rest of the pipeline is lightweight.

Pipeline

SEP and IMP share the same idea, but they push error into the encoder in two different ways.

The page should separate the two training paths clearly. They are not just renamed variants: SEP trades explicitness for speed, while IMP spends more compute to converge faster.

Step 01
Start from ordinary HD learning and compute class-wise error
Both methods begin with standard HD encoding, similarity search against class hypervectors, and class-hypervector updates. The difference is what happens next: instead of stopping after class updates, they use the resulting error signal to refine the projection matrix itself.
Step 02
SEP stochastically perturbs the projection matrix
SEP introduces a random hypermatrix B and updates the encoder using the average class error and learning rate. The key intuition is that large error should cause larger stochastic movement in the projection matrix, while low error should leave it mostly stable. The fixed and variable SEP versions differ in whether B is reused or regenerated.
Step 03
IMP performs a second forward pass with an error-modulated input
IMP projects the class error back into the input space through a random matrix, forms a modulated input, re-encodes that input, and updates the projection matrix from the difference between original and modulated hypervector activations. It is more expensive than SEP, but it gives the encoder a more targeted learning signal.
Step 04
Exploit the lower dimensions in hardware
The paper also studies acceleration. On FPGA, SEP reuses BRAM-stored random vectors through circular shifts to reduce memory traffic, while on GPU it generates random numbers directly inside CUDA kernels. The broader point is that better encoders do not just improve accuracy; they make smaller, faster HD systems possible.

Why SEP is interesting

SEP gives only implicit feedback to the encoder, which is why its very early learning looks noisy. But that also keeps the procedure simple and efficient, and the paper shows it still converges to representations that rival much heavier alternatives.

Why IMP is different

IMP is closer to a deliberate representation-correction step. By encoding the error-modulated input in a second pass, it uses more computation than SEP but reaches strong accuracy faster during training.

Results

The main result is not only higher accuracy, but better accuracy at much smaller dimension.

That distinction matters. A dynamic encoder is only valuable if it changes the accuracy-efficiency frontier, and the paper argues that SEP and IMP do exactly that.

What the paper shows about the tradeoff among Baseline, TrainableHD, SEP, and IMP

Method Key strength Main tradeoff described by the paper
Static baseline encoders Simple and lightweight training. Need much larger dimensions to reach competitive accuracy because the encoder never improves.
TrainableHD Dynamic encoder with strong accuracy. Uses backpropagation-like updates and heavier computation, which the paper treats as less biologically plausible and less efficient.
SEP fixed / variable High efficiency with strong final accuracy, especially attractive for hardware acceleration. Early training is noisier because the encoder receives stochastic, implicit feedback rather than a tightly targeted update.
IMP Fast convergence with high final accuracy. Requires a second encoding pass and therefore more computation than SEP during training.

The paper's core efficiency claim comes from dimensionality, not only training speed

Training speedups are meaningful, especially for SEP, but the bigger systems result is that strong performance is reached at D = 3,000 rather than D = 10,000. That cuts the cost of every downstream similarity search and inference pass.

SEP and IMP split the design space cleanly

SEP is the lighter, more hardware-friendly option and IMP is the more targeted, faster-converging option. The paper does a good job showing that these are complementary design points rather than one simply dominating the other.

The acceleration section is part of the contribution

The BRAM reuse strategy for SEP on FPGA and the CUDA-side random generation on GPU show that the authors are not treating forward-only learning as purely algorithmic. They are already thinking about how the training rule maps to actual accelerators.

Takeaways

Why this page matters within CELL's HD line.

  1. Point 01
    This work extends CELL's HD research from efficient inference into efficient representation learning. The encoder is no longer a frozen front-end; it becomes part of what the model learns.
  2. Point 02
    The forward-only angle is important. The paper is trying to improve HD accuracy without giving up the lightweight, brain-inspired character that makes HD attractive in the first place.
  3. Point 03
    For the website, this should read as both an algorithm paper and a deployment paper. The value is not just new training rules, but new training rules that make smaller and faster HD systems viable.
