Abstract

The interesting part of this paper is not that HDC works for robotics in principle. It is that the authors make it survive real LiDAR, real imbalance, and real robot deployment.

Earlier HDC robotics papers were mostly proof-of-concept studies in simulation, toy environments, or highly simplified sensing setups. ReactHD goes after the harder version of the problem: a real wheeled robot with continuous LiDAR streams, large feature count, temporal dependence, and the need to make reliable action decisions under tight compute and power budgets.

The framework is built around three pieces. First, a circular encoding scheme captures the geometry and temporal structure of LiDAR measurements. Second, HDC-IL uses iterative retraining and hard-negative mining to handle action imbalance in imitation learning. Third, HDC-RL adapts prioritized replay to HDC so reinforcement learning stays sample-efficient in high-dimensional robotic environments. The result is a full perception-to-action stack that achieves DNN-like learning quality while running much more efficiently on a Raspberry Pi-based robot.

Motivation

Three real-world robotics issues force ReactHD to be more than a direct port of standard HDC learning.

The paper becomes much stronger once you see that each design decision maps to a concrete failure mode in real deployment: sensor structure, action imbalance, and RL sample waste.

Real LiDAR data are continuous, correlated, and temporal

A 720-beam LiDAR stream is not a small set of independent symbolic inputs. Neighboring angles are related, the endpoints wrap around in a circle, and the control decision depends on several recent scans. The encoder therefore has to capture geometry and time, not just token identity.

Imitation learning on robots is dominated by hard negatives

In safety-focused data collection, the stop action appears far more often than motion actions. The paper reports that stop is on average 7.33 times more frequent than each non-stop action, which makes naive training drift toward overly conservative behavior unless difficult negatives are surfaced deliberately.

Reinforcement learning needs sample efficiency, not just eventual convergence

LiDAR-based state spaces are much larger than the small environments often used in prior HDC-RL work. If the replay buffer treats all transitions equally, training spends too much time on low-information experiences and fails to learn difficult navigation tasks quickly enough.

Pipeline

ReactHD combines a LiDAR-aware encoder with two lightweight learning modes that share the same deployment form.

That last point matters. The paper does not build one representation for imitation learning and another for reinforcement learning. It keeps the action-hypervector interface consistent so the model can be reused across task types.

Step 01
Encode LiDAR with circular structure and temporal order
The robot receives F = 720 LiDAR features per scan and uses L = 7 successive scans. ReactHD builds pivot hypervectors, interpolates neighboring feature positions so nearby angles remain similar, preserves the wraparound nature of the sensor, and then injects temporal ordering through permutation across recent scans.
Step 02
Train HDC-IL with iterative retraining over nine actions
For imitation learning, the system learns nine action hypervectors corresponding to eight movement directions plus stop. Unlike earlier single-pass HDC robotics work, ReactHD revisits the dataset over multiple epochs and updates both the predicted and true action hypervectors when a sample is misclassified.
Step 03
Mine hard negatives to prevent the policy from collapsing into stop-heavy behavior
Before each training iteration, the current action model scores negative-class samples and assigns weights based on how difficult they are to classify correctly. Weighted sampling then injects those hard negatives into retraining so the model learns when it is safe to move instead of simply over-predicting stop.
Step 04
Train HDC-RL with dual action models and surprise-based replay
The RL path maintains an online action model and a target model, estimates Q-values with dot products in hyperspace, and stores transitions in a replay buffer. Prioritized replay is implemented through an HDC-friendly surprise factor based on the gap between predicted and target Q-values, so training focuses on informative experiences.
Step 05
Deploy the same lightweight action model on an actual robot
The experiments use a Wheeltec Mecanum omnidirectional robot with Raspberry Pi 4 and an RPLidar A1 sensor, evaluated on obstacle avoidance, object tracking, and navigation tasks. This is where the paper's strongest claim lands: the method is not left at offline accuracy plots but tested on a real platform.

Why circular encoding is more than a pre-processing trick

The encoder is doing the job that makes HDC plausible for robotics. Without respecting continuity, neighboring angles, and temporal order, the high-dimensional model would be forced to learn control from a distorted view of the sensor field.

Why the unified IL and RL structure is useful

Both HDC-IL and HDC-RL end in action hypervectors that can be deployed with the same lightweight similarity-based inference. That keeps the control stack simple and makes it easier to switch between demonstration-driven and autonomous learning regimes.

Results

The paper's strongest evidence is that efficiency gains survive the move from toy tasks to real LiDAR robotics.

ReactHD is not claiming to dominate DNNs on every metric. It is claiming something more valuable for embedded robotics: comparable learning quality with dramatically lighter training and inference on edge hardware.

What the ablations say about where ReactHD's gains come from

Component Reported evidence Interpretation
Circular encoding Removing circular encoding causes a 12.5% accuracy drop on average across the four LiDAR tasks. The geometry of the sensor field is not incidental. Encoding nearby angles as related is central to learning usable control behavior.
Hard-negative mining The ablation reports a 5.8% improvement on the TRA task when hard-negative mining is used. Stop-heavy class imbalance is a real deployment issue, and the training loop needs explicit mechanisms to surface difficult non-stop decisions.
Prioritized replay in HDC-RL Without PER, the simplified agent learns more slowly and fails to solve NAV2 within 1000 epochs in the reported study. Sample efficiency is what makes RL plausible in larger LiDAR environments; uniform replay is not enough.
ReactHD vs DNN The paper reports comparable learning quality to the DNN baseline across the target tasks while improving speed and energy on Raspberry Pi. The value proposition is not beating DNNs outright, but preserving task quality under much tighter deployment budgets.

The paper expands HDC robotics from proof-of-concept to actual deployment

The authors explicitly position ReactHD as the first HDC-based framework deployed in real-world settings. That matters because prior work often stayed in simulation or offline evaluation, where sensor structure and control latency are easier to abstract away.

The efficiency story is tied to the hardware target, not a generic benchmark machine

Running on a Raspberry Pi 4 with an RPLidar A1 keeps the evaluation honest. The 14.2x speedup and 15.3x energy improvement mean more when the model is measured on the kind of constrained robot computer that would actually carry it.

ReactHD succeeds because it fixes the representation and the learning loop together

A better encoder alone would not solve replay inefficiency, and prioritized replay alone would not fix distorted sensor representations. The paper works because circular encoding, hard-negative mining, and HDC-RL are aligned to the same robotics constraints.

Takeaways

Why this page matters inside CELL's embedded HD line.

  1. Point 01
    ReactHD is the page that makes CELL's HDC work feel operational in robotics. It is not only about a better learning rule, but about carrying that rule onto a real mobile platform with real sensors.
  2. Point 02
    The paper is especially useful for the website because it spans both imitation learning and reinforcement learning while keeping a common deployment form. That makes the project broader than a single-task robotics demo.
  3. Point 03
    For readers, the core message should be clear: embedded symbolic learning can stay lightweight without becoming toy-scale. ReactHD shows that HDC can support real sensorimotor control when the encoder and training loop are designed around deployment realities.
