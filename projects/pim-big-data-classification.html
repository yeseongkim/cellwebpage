Abstract

A programmable PIM system for non-arithmetic-heavy classification.

CHOIR starts from a practical critique of prior processing-in-memory designs. Near-data accelerators still spend area and energy on extra CMOS logic, while many in-memory processors are excellent at vector arithmetic but awkward for workloads dominated by conditionals, branching, and tree-style decision logic.

This paper proposes Classification Hypothesis Online Identifier on Resistive memory, or CHOIR, a ReRAM-based PIM architecture built from digital-friendly CAM search and in-memory NOR operations instead of large ADC and DAC blocks. On top of that hardware, the paper rewrites a full tree-boosting workflow using programmable PIM APIs, including genetic sample selection, decision-tree learning, and AdaBoost-style reweighting. The result is a storage-class architecture that delivers GPU-level classification quality while improving training performance and energy by 123x and 52x over XGBoost on GPU.

Motivation

Why CHOIR had to be both programmable and memory-native.

The paper is not just another PIM datapath. It is explicitly designed for workloads where comparisons, rule evaluation, and data movement dominate, which makes a generic arithmetic-only in-memory engine insufficient.

Data movement was the first enemy

Big-data classification pipelines move huge datasets between memory and processors even though much of the computation is simple branching and accumulation. CHOIR tries to keep both training and inference close to storage.

Analog PIM paid too much area tax

Earlier analog or mixed-signal in-memory processors relied on large peripherals such as ADC and DAC blocks. The paper highlights that these can dominate chip area and undercut the density needed for storage-class memory.

Tree boosting needs conditionals, not only MACs

AdaBoost and decision-tree training rely heavily on less-than checks, search, count, and selective updates. CHOIR is designed so those operations are first-class citizens inside memory rather than awkward host-side fallbacks.

Pipeline

How CHOIR turns a ReRAM array into a tree-boosting engine.

The system is best understood as a hardware-software stack: a programmable ISA on top of low-overhead in-memory primitives, then a tree-boosting algorithm rewritten to exploit those primitives instead of fighting them.

Step 01
Build PIM from CAM search and in-memory NOR
CHOIR uses a CAM-style parallel bit search for row-parallel conditional operations and MAGIC-style in-memory NOR for column-parallel arithmetic and logic. This avoids the expensive analog peripheral overhead found in other in-memory processors.
Step 02
Expose the array through a programmable ISA
The architecture lets software view memory blocks as arrays and invoke PIM instructions such as less-than, count, add, multiply, increment, copy, and cross-block write. Selection and computation are loosely separated so massively parallel operands can be targeted efficiently.
Step 03
Select representative samples with GA
CHOIR first uses a genetic algorithm on chromosome blocks to choose feature values that mimic the full data distribution. The fitness metric is mean absolute deviation, computed directly with in-memory less-than, count, and addition operations.
Step 04
Train decision stumps and boost them
The selected samples drive decision-tree learning inside the data-storage blocks. Entropy-based split evaluation, node-ID updates, and sample reweighting for SAMME.R-style boosting are all rewritten into the PIM programming model.
Step 05
Run inference with the same in-memory rule flow
Online inference reuses row-parallel comparisons and node-ID updates to traverse the stored decision trees for many samples at once, then aggregates class probabilities across the boosted ensemble.
Results

CHOIR keeps competitive accuracy while removing the cost of moving data out to the GPU.

The paper evaluates CHOIR on 13 datasets and compares it with scikit-learn, an OpenCL version of the same software flow, GPU XGBoost, and DNN baselines. The main story is that CHOIR stays accurate while shifting the computational bottleneck back into memory.

Representative findings from the ICCAD 2021 paper

Theme Reported result Why it matters
Area efficiency CHOIR leaves 96 percent of the block area to the ReRAM array itself, with logic and latch support consuming only a small additional fraction. The design remains plausible as storage-class memory rather than becoming a logic-heavy accelerator disguised as memory.
Training efficiency Compared with GPU implementations, CHOIR improves training energy and performance on average by 153x and 63x over the OpenCL baseline and 123x and 52x over XGBoost. The biggest win comes from avoiding repeated host-memory and GPU-memory traffic.
Inference efficiency For inference, the paper reports average energy and performance improvements of 83x and 11x over the OpenCL baseline, and 82x and 7x over XGBoost. The system is not just fast to train; the learned ensemble also executes efficiently inside the PIM architecture.
Quality retention Across datasets such as SHUTTLE, PAGE, CARDIO, PAMAP2, UCIHAR, ISOLET, MNIST, FACE, HIGGS, and SUSY, CHOIR stays competitive with or better than conventional ML baselines. The architecture is solving the compute-location problem without collapsing model quality.

A programmable memory-systems page

CHOIR is one of the strongest early examples on the site of CELL pushing computation into memory without surrendering programmability. The ISA and runtime are as important as the array circuit choices.

Why it is not just a MAC engine

The paper's distinguishing feature is support for conditional logic, selection, and tree-style rule evaluation. That makes it relevant for non-arithmetic-centric machine learning workloads where many prior PIM designs struggle.

Takeaways

Why this page matters in CELL's systems thread.
