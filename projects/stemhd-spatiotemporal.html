Abstract

Treating nearby features as related instead of accidentally orthogonal.

Classic HDC encoders are attractive because they are simple, but they usually assign independent random base hypervectors to every feature position. That assumption is wasteful for structured signals such as sensor streams, spectrogram-like features, or images, where neighboring positions are often correlated. In those cases the encoder throws away useful spatiotemporal information before learning even begins.

STEM-HD reframes the encoder as the main opportunity for improvement. It generates correlated base hypervectors so nearby positions stay similar in hyperspace, plugs that mechanism into existing HDC encoders without adding runtime overhead, and then exploits the resulting regularity for hardware reuse. The result is a cleaner representation for structured data and a faster accelerator path for training and inference.

Motivation

Why structured data was a weak spot for earlier HDC encoders.

The paper is not trying to redesign the entire HDC pipeline. It targets one assumption that had quietly become expensive: treating every feature index as unrelated to every other one.

Random positions erase continuity

Earlier encoders map each feature position to a random base hypervector. That makes adjacent time steps, neighboring pixels, or nearby frequency bins nearly orthogonal even when the original signal is smooth and strongly correlated.

Large dimensions become a workaround

Because the encoder ignores positional structure, prior HDC systems often rely on very high dimensions just to maintain enough separability. The paper explicitly frames that as representation waste rather than an unavoidable property of HDC.

Encoding dominates the hardware budget

On the Kintex-7 FPGA baseline used in the paper, encoding takes 10.6x more runtime than the rest of inference. If the encoder stays random and irregular, the accelerator has little opportunity to reuse work.

Pipeline

How STEM-HD changes the encoder without breaking the rest of HDC.

The important design choice is compatibility. STEM-HD is introduced as a new base-hypervector generator that can be dropped into existing HDC encoders rather than a complete replacement for training, retraining, or inference.

Step 01
Generate correlated base hypervectors
Instead of sampling every position independently, STEM-HD creates partition hypervectors and interpolates between them so nearby feature indices produce similar base hypervectors. The paper presents this both for 1D sequences and for 2D feature layouts.
Step 02
Preserve spatiotemporal distance during encoding
Once the base hypervectors are correlated, shifted or neighboring patterns in the original feature space remain closer in hyperspace. That directly fixes the failure mode where two nearly identical structured signals were projected too far apart by random position codes.
Step 03
Reuse existing HDC learning rules
Initial training, retraining, and inference still follow the normal HDC class-memory flow. The paper emphasizes that STEM-HD modifies how base hypervectors are created, not the lightweight learning procedure that makes HDC appealing for embedded systems.
Step 04
Exploit duplicated components in hardware
Because neighboring base hypervectors now share structure, the accelerator can reuse computations that would otherwise be recomputed independently. That is the bridge from a better representation to the reported runtime and energy savings on FPGA.
Results

The win is modestly better accuracy plus a much cleaner hardware story.

STEM-HD is evaluated on structured-data workloads including human activity recognition, emotion recognition, and position recognition. The paper argues that the new encoder converges faster, tolerates smaller dimensions better, and creates the regularity needed for practical reuse in an FPGA implementation.

Representative findings reported in the paper

Theme Reported result Meaning
Structured encoding STEM-HD explicitly models positional correlation for 1D and 2D feature layouts instead of assigning fully random position codes. The method is aimed at the right failure mode for sensor and spatial data rather than only tuning later stages of the classifier.
Dimension robustness The paper reports that STEM-HD is more robust to dimensionality reduction than the state-of-the-art baseline encoders. That matters for embedded deployment because the benefit is not tied to keeping a very large hypervector budget.
Hardware integration The encoder is designed to integrate with existing HDC encoding methods while adding no extra runtime overhead to the baseline inference flow. The contribution is practical: better structure awareness without abandoning the low-cost HDC pipeline.
FPGA deployment The implementation is demonstrated on a Kintex-7 FPGA KC705 platform across multiple structured-data workloads. This positions the work as an algorithm-and-accelerator co-design paper, not just a representation paper.

Why this page matters in CELL's HD line

This paper is an early example of CELL treating the encoder as the real design surface. Later work in the lab keeps pushing trainable, forward-only, or hardware-aware encoding, and STEM-HD is one of the clearest first steps in that direction.

What changed architecturally

The novelty is not a heavier classifier. STEM-HD keeps HDC lightweight, but stops pretending that neighboring features are unrelated. That one change improves learning quality and makes computation reuse possible on hardware.

Takeaways

Why STEM-HD deserves a standalone project page.

  1. Point 01
    STEM-HD shows that HDC accuracy and efficiency can both improve when the encoder respects the geometry of the original data instead of randomizing it away.
  2. Point 02
    The page should read as a joint representation-and-systems result. The spatiotemporal encoder matters because it creates reusable structure for the accelerator, not only because it changes the math.
  3. Point 03
    Chronologically, this is a strong anchor for the lab's later work on smarter HD encoders, lower-dimensional deployment, and practical edge hardware mappings.
