DUAL: Acceleration of Clustering Algorithms using Digital-based Processing In-Memory

DUAL recasts clustering around binary hypervectors and digital crossbar search, so hierarchical clustering, k-means, and DBSCAN can run inside memory without the ADC/DAC-heavy overhead of analog PIM.

Abstract

Making unsupervised clustering look like a memory problem, not a data-movement problem.

Clustering is dominated by pairwise distance computation, nearest-neighbor search, and repeated updates over large data sets. On conventional processors that means moving large volumes of data between compute and memory. Existing analog PIM designs help with dot products, but they are a poor fit for the full clustering pipeline and pay a heavy price in ADC and DAC overhead.

DUAL changes both the algorithm and the hardware interface. It first maps data points into binary hypervectors so similarity can be evaluated with Hamming distance instead of Euclidean distance, then executes the core clustering operations directly inside digital crossbar memories. The result is a PIM architecture that supports search and arithmetic in place, handles multiple popular clustering algorithms, and scales better than a GPU baseline on large workloads.

Motivation

Why clustering was a bad match for both CPUs and prior PIM designs.

The paper is not just proposing "PIM for clustering." It starts by showing why the usual clustering kernels and the usual PIM assumptions do not line up cleanly.

Pairwise distance dominates runtime

Hierarchical clustering, DBSCAN, and k-means repeatedly compare points or cluster centers across large data sets. That creates heavy pairwise distance traffic and nearest-search pressure before any useful structure has been extracted.

Analog PIM is not enough

Earlier PIM accelerators were mostly designed around analog dot products. The paper points out that clustering needs search, Hamming similarity, distance updates, and control-heavy memory interaction that analog PIM does not support cleanly.

ADC and DAC overhead breaks efficiency

The authors highlight that ADC and DAC blocks can dominate area and power in prior PIM systems, with one cited class of DNN accelerators spending 98 percent of chip power and area there. DUAL explicitly avoids that tax by staying digital.

Pipeline

How DUAL turns clustering into binary search plus in-memory updates.

The important move is algorithm-hardware co-design. DUAL would not work by dropping a standard Euclidean clustering kernel onto PIM, so the paper first changes the representation and then builds the memory operations around it.

Step 01
Map original data into binary hypervectors
DUAL's HD-Mapper projects each feature vector with random Gaussian base vectors, applies a cosine-based nonlinearity, and binarizes the result. That preserves neighborhood structure well enough to replace Euclidean distance with the PIM-friendly Hamming metric.
Step 02
Use data and distance blocks inside digital crossbars
Data blocks store encoded points and perform row-parallel Hamming distance computation. Distance blocks store pairwise distances and support nearest search plus arithmetic updates. The paper presents a modified CAM-style search flow with non-linear sampling to support 7-bit Hamming windows and exact nearest-value search.
Step 03
Support multiple clustering algorithms with the same primitives
Hierarchical clustering runs by computing pairwise similarities, searching for the nearest pair, and updating linkage distances in memory. The same search and arithmetic blocks also support DBSCAN and k-means with different controller flows.
Step 04
Scale through pipelining, interconnects, and parallel copies
DUAL organizes tiles of crossbar memories with bit-serial, row-parallel transfers between neighboring blocks. Interconnects and counter blocks keep Hamming computation and distance updates moving, while extra copies of encoded data trade area for more clustering throughput.
Results

The quality stays close to conventional clustering while the hardware efficiency jumps.

The paper evaluates DUAL on hierarchical clustering, DBSCAN, and k-means over both synthetic and real data sets, compares against GPU implementations, and also checks how much quality is lost when the hyperspace dimension is reduced for more speed.

Theme Reported result Meaning
Quality retention At D = 4000, DUAL reports comparable clustering quality to Euclidean baselines: +1.2 percent for hierarchical clustering, +0.4 percent for DBSCAN, and -1.3 percent for k-means on average. The binary hypervector mapping is not just a speed hack. It keeps enough structure to make the clustering outcome competitive.
Algorithm-level gains Hierarchical clustering reaches 67.1x speedup and 328.7x energy efficiency over GPU; DBSCAN reaches 71.7x and 293.3x; k-means reaches 37.5x and 131.6x. Search-heavy algorithms benefit the most because DUAL's in-memory Hamming and nearest-search operations are much stronger than its slower NOR-based arithmetic path.
Dimension-quality tradeoff With only 1 percent quality loss, hierarchical clustering reaches 90.6x speedup and 443.9x energy efficiency; at 2 percent loss, the same paper reports 116.7x and 572.2x. The hyperspace dimension becomes a direct system knob for quality versus performance.
Design importance Removing interconnects slows hierarchical clustering by 3.9x, and removing counters slows the algorithms by about 2.1x to 2.7x on average. The paper's architecture details are not incidental. The counters and local data movement path are central to the final efficiency numbers.

Why this is a foundational systems page

DUAL is one of the clearest early examples of CELL's systems work going beyond accelerator placement and into representation design. The HD-Mapper is what makes the digital PIM architecture possible in the first place.

What makes it different from generic PIM

The paper is not simply "clustering on memory." It introduces a digital search-and-update substrate that can support nearest search, Hamming computation, and linkage updates together, which is why it can cover hierarchical clustering, DBSCAN, and k-means in one framework.

Takeaways

Why DUAL belongs in CELL's project-page set.

  1. Point 01
    DUAL shows how a clustering workload can be reformulated around hardware-native operations instead of trying to force Euclidean kernels onto an ill-suited memory substrate.
  2. Point 02
    The page matters because it connects representation learning, digital PIM, and scalable unsupervised algorithms in one system rather than treating them as separate optimization layers.
  3. Point 03
    Chronologically, this is an early anchor for the lab's memory-centric accelerator thread and for later work where algorithm structure is deliberately shaped around the target hardware.
